The measurement spectrum sub-analysis is the framing engine for everything else in `field_benchmarks/`. It exists to answer the question researchers and policymakers always ask: "Your 40% number is much higher than Iceberg's 11.7% — how can both be right?" They can both be right because they're measuring genuinely different things, and this analysis makes that explicit.

*Full detail: [theoretical_vs_confirmed_report.md](theoretical_vs_confirmed_report.md)*

## The Three Layers

![Measurement spectrum: all sources on one axis](../questions/field_benchmarks/theoretical_vs_confirmed/figures/measurement_spectrum.png)

The spectrum runs from confirmed real-world usage at the bottom of the measurement hierarchy up through deployment-constrained readiness to technical capability ceilings. These aren't rungs of the same ladder — they're answers to different questions:

**Layer 1 — Confirmed usage (what AI is actually doing):** Our all_confirmed at 40%, AEI's occupation-coverage analysis (~36% of occupations with confirmed >=25% task coverage), and ChatGPT usage studies (~27% of sessions work-related). These all measure observed behavior, not potential.

**Layer 2 — Deployment-constrained readiness (what orgs could roll out today):** Seampoint's 20% takeover / 51% augment estimates for Utah. These ask a policy and operations question: if you wanted to redeploy workers, how much could governance actually support right now? The 20% figure isn't a capability floor — it's a deployment constraint.

**Layer 3 — Technical capability ceiling (what AI tools can technically do):** Project Iceberg's 11.7% Full Index. This measures skill-value substitutability: of all the wage value generated by worker skills, what fraction maps to skills AI tools can technically replicate? The 11.7% sounds low, but it's a conservative analytical question (skill wage value, not task breadth), and it's measuring a different thing than usage.

## Why Confirmed > Technical Capability

![Three-layer grouped bar comparison](../questions/field_benchmarks/theoretical_vs_confirmed/figures/layer_breakdown.png)

Our 40% exceeds Iceberg's 11.7% not because we're measuring more generously — it's because task-level usage breadth and skill-wage substitutability are different dimensions. When an LLM helps a financial analyst draft a report, that interaction may touch many tasks but not necessarily substitute the core skill value that defines the analyst's role. Iceberg is asking whether AI can replace what makes that worker valuable; we're asking whether AI is involved in what that worker does day to day. Both are important. Neither supersedes the other.

## Confirmed and Deployment-Constrained Alignment

The alignment between our agentic_confirmed (20.3%) and Seampoint's takeover rate (20%) is a genuinely useful cross-validation: the AI tasks that an organization could currently hand off to AI fully (governance-constrained) roughly correspond to what we observe in confirmed agentic tool-use. Our ceiling (50.3%) and Seampoint's augment estimate (51%) similarly converge. This isn't coincidence — it suggests both frameworks are capturing something real about the same underlying deployment reality, just from different angles.

## Confirmed Methodology Note

AEI (Humlum & Vestergaard, 2024) is the confirmed-usage source most directly comparable to ours — both are derived from actual AI system logs cross-walked to O*NET occupational data. Their finding that 57% of AI task attempts are augmentative (vs. 43% automative) is consistent with our framework's auto-augmentation logic: most AI usage involves AI extending human judgment, not replacing it.
