# Pivot Distance: Zone-Level Skill and Knowledge Breakdowns

For each job zone, four charts show the specific elements that drive the pivot cost — the skills and knowledge a high-risk worker must gain to reach the profile of a typical low-risk occupation in the same zone, and the elements they would leave behind.

**Gained** = elements where the low-risk target profile exceeds the high-risk starting point (the binding acquisition cost).  
**Dropped** = elements where the high-risk starting point exceeds the low-risk target (skills the worker currently has that are less relevant in the pivot destination).

All values are in absolute importance × level units (the same scale as the pivot cost totals in the main report).

## Zone 2: Some Preparation

Zone 2 (181.06 total cost, 15 gap elements) is where the pattern becomes stark: service-sector workers pivoting to trades face a concentrated wall of construction and mechanical knowledge they've never been asked to develop. The 98.6% AI-assistance rate means most of the knowledge acquisition is AI-teachable — but the hands-on practice still requires real-world training.

## Zone 3: Medium Preparation (The Crisis Zone)

Zone 3 is the most expensive pivot in the analysis (359.20 total cost, 41 gap elements). HR assistants, bookkeepers, and brokerage clerks face a broad-spectrum knowledge restructuring across mechanical, physics, engineering, and construction domains. It's not one big gap — it's a dozen medium-sized ones stacked together. The 97.8% AI-assistance rate is notable: even here, almost every gap element is one where AI capability exceeds the high-risk worker's current level, making AI a viable tutoring partner for the knowledge phase.

## Zone 4: Considerable Preparation

Zone 4 (262.91 total cost, 39 gap elements) has a comparable number of gap elements to Zone 3 but each gap is smaller on average. The pivot runs through applied science and logistics domains — physics, mechanics, transportation knowledge. Professional-level workers pivoting to lower-risk roles in the same credential tier need to redirect rather than rebuild their knowledge base. AI-assistance rate drops to 91.2%, reflecting the increasing depth and specialization of the target occupations.

## Zone 5: Extensive Preparation

Zone 5 (234.61 total cost, 32 gap elements) is surprisingly moderate. High-risk academics and low-risk clinicians share enough intellectual infrastructure — research methods, analytical reasoning, statistical literacy — that the pivot is less about building new foundations and more about redirecting existing ones. But the specific gaps reveal the real challenge: psychology, therapy/counseling, and clinical domains require not just knowledge but supervised clinical practice and licensure. The AI-assistance rate drops further to 86.0% here.
