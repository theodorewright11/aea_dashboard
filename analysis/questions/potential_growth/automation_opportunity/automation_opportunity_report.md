# Automation Opportunity: Where AI Leads and the Gap Is Largest

*Config: all_confirmed (SKA + adoption gap baseline) | Ceiling: all_ceiling | Method: freq | Auto-aug ON | National | 8 flags, max 10 | Risk tiers: 8-10 high, 5-7 mod-high, 3-4 mod-low, 0-2 low*

---

96 occupations (out of 893 analyzed) fall into Q1: AI capability already exceeds typical occupational skill need (SKA overall_pct > 100%) AND the adoption gap is large (ceiling minus confirmed > median 7.3pp). These are the highest-value automation targets available with today's tools. 33 of those 96 also carry a high risk tier from the job risk scoring analysis, meaning they're not just automation opportunities — they're occupations where the combination of AI capability, deployment gap, and structural vulnerability signals likely job transformation rather than just augmentation. The rest (63 occupations in Q1 without high risk) are more straightforwardly opportunity: AI can reach them, the tools aren't deployed, and transformation pressure is lower.

---

## The Quadrant Framework

Every occupation lands in one of four quadrants defined by two splits: SKA overall_pct at 100% (the natural threshold — AI as % of occupational need, where >100% means AI leads) and the median adoption gap (7.3pp).

**Q1 — Automation opportunity (96 occupations):** AI capability exceeds what the occupation needs (SKA overall_pct > 100%) AND confirmed adoption is well below ceiling (big gap). This is where the economic opportunity is most legible: the tools work, and they're not being used.

**Q2 — Already adopted (62 occupations):** AI leads on SKA (>100%), but the adoption gap is small. These occupations have largely converged on their AI-accessible ceiling already.

**Q3 — Tool gap (350 occupations):** Humans still have the skills advantage (SKA < 100%) but there's a meaningful adoption gap. The ceiling suggests AI could do more, but workers in these roles have distinct capabilities that AI tools haven't fully matched.

**Q4 — Low priority (385 occupations):** Small adoption gap AND humans lead (<100%). Limited AI capability and limited gap — these occupations are relatively insulated from either AI displacement or augmentation at current capability levels.

The distribution is heavily weighted toward the human-advantage quadrants (Q3 + Q4 = 735 occupations, 82%). Only 158 occupations (Q1 + Q2) are in territory where AI leads on skills above 100% — a much more selective threshold than the old median-based split. Of those with AI advantage, 96 still have meaningful deployment gaps remaining (Q1).

## The Transformation Signal

When Q1 occupations also carry a high risk tier, the picture changes from "economic opportunity" to "structural change signal." 33 of the 96 Q1 occupations meet this threshold.

![Q1 + High Risk: Transformation Signal Occupations](figures/transformation_signal.png)

These occupations have AI capability exceeding human occupational need (SKA overall_pct > 100%), a meaningful deployment gap, and structural vulnerability markers (low job zone, below-average outlook, or high software content). The combination doesn't guarantee job loss — it doesn't even predict it directly. But it's the set of occupations where the three pressure points converge: AI can do the work, it isn't being deployed yet, and when it is, the workers in those roles are less insulated from the displacement effects.

Top transformation signal occupations:
- **Retail Salespersons**: SKA 110%, 11.6pp gap, high risk. The AI capability exceeds occupational need, the deployment gap is real, and the structural position of retail sales (low job zone, large workforce) makes them exposed when deployment happens.
- **Office Clerks, General**: SKA 113%, 11.1pp gap, high risk. A broad clerical category with AI capability meaningfully above the 100% threshold and risk score of 10 (maximum).
- **Bookkeeping, Accounting, and Auditing Clerks**: SKA 106%, 13.2pp gap, high risk. A 13pp gap means confirmed usage has significant room to close toward the ceiling. Their outlook and job zone are both in the at-risk range.

What Q1 + high-risk does NOT mean: that these jobs are going away imminently, or that AI deployment will automatically cause displacement. Deployment decisions are made by organizations, not by capability alone. But the combination is a meaningful early indicator.

At the sector level, the scatter shows Office/Admin and Sales as the clearest opportunity sectors: both have above-median adoption gaps AND above-100% mean SKA (AI leads at the sector level). Transportation/Moving has a high adoption gap (13.2pp) but below-100% SKA (87%, humans still have an edge in aggregate). Computer occupations have a below-100% SKA (88%) but a 10pp adoption gap — suggesting that even in tech, deployment hasn't caught up to capability.

The sectors with the strongest combination — high adoption gap AND AI SKA above 100% — are the ones where economic opportunity and transformation pressure overlap most directly. Office/Admin (mean SKA 105%, mean adoption gap 16.3pp) and Sales (mean SKA 102%, mean adoption gap 11.9pp) are the clearest candidates.

Management's mean SKA of 84% is worth noting: despite the large adoption gap (15.4pp), humans still maintain a clear aggregate skills advantage in management roles. This suggests the ceiling gap in Management is more about task-level AI capability (AI can do specific tasks) than wholesale AI advantage across the skill profile. The distinction matters for how the gap closes: it's likely to close through task-level augmentation rather than replacement of the full job.

## SKA, Adoption Gap, and What This Analysis Can't Tell You

The SKA overall_pct is computed using O*NET skills/abilities/knowledge importance scores crossed with occupation-level AI exposure. A value above 100% means AI capability (proxied through pct_tasks_affected × element importance) exceeds what the occupation typically needs — AI leads. A value below 100% means humans still lead — human advantage.

A few limitations to name:

The SKA formula treats all elements as comparable in scale, which they aren't — some elements (like critical thinking) are systematically more important across jobs than others. The gap scores are relative, not absolute.

The adoption gap is measured at the national level and doesn't capture within-occupation variance. A 25pp gap for Medical Secretaries means on average there's a 25pp difference between confirmed and ceiling — but some organizations are already at the ceiling while others are far below confirmed. The "gap" is a distributional fact about current deployment, not a statement about any individual workplace.

The transformation signal (Q1 + high risk) is correlational. High-risk tiers were scored on 8 flags (max 10) including exposure trend, outlook, and job zone. Risk tiers: 8-10 = high, 5-7 = mod-high, 3-4 = mod-low, 0-2 = low. The combination with Q1 is an interpretive overlay, not a causal model of job change.
