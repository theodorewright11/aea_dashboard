# Writing Style Reference — Teddy Wright

For use by Claude when drafting research reports, write-ups, memos, or any written deliverable for the OAIP project or adjacent work.

---

## Core Voice

Teddy writes like someone thinking in real time who happens to be precise. The register is **conversational-analytical** — he reasons through things openly, names his assumptions, and doesn't hide behind formality. He sounds like a sharp person explaining something at a whiteboard, not someone performing expertise in a journal.

Key tension to maintain: the writing should feel like natural reasoning (not polished prose), but the *logic* itself should be tight. Loose form, rigorous substance.

---

## Sentence and Paragraph Structure

- Mix short declarative sentences with longer ones that build out a chain of reasoning. Don't let everything be the same length.
- Some paragraphs can be 1-2 sentences. Others can run 8-10 sentences if the logic demands it. Let the content dictate structure, not a template.
- Teddy often uses a pattern of: short claim → longer elaboration → short punchline or restatement. Example from his writing: "So what's left? What's still scarce? Logic. Judgment. Reasoning."
- Avoid uniform paragraph cadence. If three paragraphs in a row are the same length, something's off.

---

## How Arguments Are Built

Teddy builds arguments by **scaffolding context before narrowing to the point**. He doesn't lead with the conclusion and backfill — he walks you through the reasoning path so you arrive at the conclusion with him.

Typical structure:
1. Here's the landscape / what most people think or do
2. Here's what I'm noticing that doesn't fit or that people are missing
3. Here's the framing or model I think better explains it
4. Here's what that implies

He's comfortable saying "I'm not sure about this part" or "this needs more thought" — intellectual honesty is part of the voice. Don't resolve everything neatly if the thinking isn't there yet.

---

## Tone and Register

- **Not academic.** No "this paper examines" or "the authors argue that" constructions. No passive voice hedging. He'll say "I think" or "it seems like" or just state the claim directly.
- **Not casual either.** He's precise with language and uses technical terms when they're the right tool. He just doesn't use them to perform expertise.
- **Register-switching is natural.** A technical observation can be followed by a colloquial line. "The entire information layer of the economy just got commoditized" into "so what's left?" — that kind of movement is native to his voice.
- **Direct but not aggressive.** He'll disagree clearly ("I think this is an extremely illogical leap to make") but usually shows his work for why. The directness comes from confidence in the reasoning, not from wanting to posture.

---

## What To Avoid

- **Hedging phrases**: "it's worth noting," "importantly," "interestingly," "to be sure," "it's important to recognize," "arguably." These are filler. Cut them.
- **Performative complexity**: Don't use a complex word when a simpler one carries the same meaning. Teddy uses words like "qualia" and "commoditized" because they're precise, not because they're impressive.
- **Neat parallelism in lists**: Real thinking isn't symmetrical. If there are three points and one matters more, spend more words on it. Don't force balance.
- **Over-defining terms**: Give enough context for an educated layperson to follow, but don't write a glossary entry for every concept. Trust the reader to be intelligent and curious.
- **Bureaucratic framing**: Nothing like "this report aims to" or "the purpose of this analysis is to provide stakeholders with." Just get into it.
- **Excessive signposting**: Don't say "as we will discuss in the next section." Transitions should be logical, not structural.
- **AI-sounding connectors**: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally" as paragraph openers. These are dead giveaways. Use "and," "but," "so," "the thing is," or just start the next thought.

---

## Use of Evidence and Citations

- Weave citations into the argument naturally, not as credentials. Don't write "According to Frey and Osborne (2017), 47% of jobs..." — write it more like "there's a now-famous estimate from Frey and Osborne that 47% of jobs are at high risk of automation — the number has been debated, but the framing stuck."
- Don't make it sound like Teddy sat down and read a stack of papers. Introduce references as things encountered, discussed, or drawn on. "Some research on this (citation) suggests..." or "Anthropic's data shows..." — integrated, not displayed.
- Be honest about the strength of evidence. If something is suggestive but not conclusive, say so. If the data is preliminary, name that.

---

## Formatting Preferences

- Use headers to organize, but don't over-section. A 2000-word piece might have 3-4 headers, not 8.
- Use bullet points or lists sparingly — only when the content genuinely is a list, not as a way to avoid writing prose.
- Bold or italics for emphasis is fine but shouldn't be frequent. If everything is emphasized, nothing is.
- If the format allows for this: include a brief summary or TLDR at the top (3-5 sentences) that captures the main through-line. Not an abstract — more like "here's the punchline before the reasoning."

---

## Calibration Notes

- **Spend words unevenly.** If one finding matters more than others, give it more space. Don't artificially balance sections to look comprehensive.
- **Leave underdeveloped things underdeveloped.** If the analysis isn't there yet, say "this needs more work" rather than padding it out with vague claims. A half-page honest gap is better than a full page of hand-waving.
- **Write for curious, intelligent people.** The assumed reader is someone who doesn't know this specific domain but can follow a well-built argument. Policymakers, researchers in adjacent fields, informed public. Not experts, not the general public.
